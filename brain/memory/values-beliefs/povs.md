## Core [Field] Points of View

### POV 1: Most [Field] Problems Are People Problems, Not Tool Problems

> "Companies think they need better software. Usually they need better processes and clearer expectations."

**My Take**: 80% of [Field] challenges are caused by unclear processes, poor training, or misaligned incentives - not inadequate tools.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "We need to implement [new support platform] to fix our [Field] issues."

**Why They're Wrong**:
- New tools can't fix broken processes
- Training gaps don't disappear with software changes
- Tool migrations create disruption without addressing root causes
- Companies invest in platforms when they should invest in people and process

**The Evidence**:
- Clients I've worked with who fixed process improved CSAT without changing tools
- Tool migrations that failed because underlying issues weren't addressed
- Support teams with mediocre tools but great process outperforming teams with fancy tools and no process

**Implications for My Work**:
- Start assessment with process and people, not tool evaluation
- Recommend process improvement before tool changes
- Tool selection comes after understanding workflow needs

---

### POV 2: Tiered Support Is Overrated for Early-Stage SaaS

> "All-hands support isn't a bug at 50 customers - it's a feature."

**My Take**: Companies rush to implement tiered support (L1/L2/L3) too early, losing the product insight and customer proximity that drives early-stage innovation.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "You need tiered support to scale efficiently."

**Why They're Not Entirely Right**:
- All-hands support keeps entire company customer-aware
- Product and engineering learn directly from customer pain
- Hiring specialized support too early creates information silos
- The transition point is later than most think (150-200 customers, not 50)

**The Evidence**:
- Companies that maintained all-hands until 150+ customers had better product-market fit
- Early tiering often results in support team unable to answer complex questions
- Engineering teams disconnected from customers build worse products

**The Nuance**:
- Tiering is essential eventually (200+ customers, definitely)
- The key is knowing when, not whether
- Transition should be gradual, not overnight

**Implications for My Work**:
- Push back when Series A companies want tiered support at 30 customers
- Help identify the right transition point based on volume and complexity
- Design hybrid models that preserve product feedback loops

### POV 3: CSAT Alone Is a Vanity Metric

> "A happy customer who churned is still gone."

**My Take**: CSAT without operational metrics and retention data is nearly useless for driving business outcomes.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "High CSAT means our [Field] is working."

**Why That's Incomplete**:
- CSAT doesn't predict retention or expansion
- Can be high while resolution time tanks and customers quietly churn
- Easy to game (only send surveys after easy wins)
- Doesn't capture opportunity cost of support inefficiency

**What To Measure Instead**:
- CSAT + Resolution Time + Customer Health Score + Retention
- Leading indicators (response time, escalation rate) + lagging indicators (CSAT, churn)
- Operational efficiency (tickets per customer, self-service rate)
- Business impact (support cost per customer, expansion rate post-support)

**Implications for My Work**:
- Assessment always includes operational metrics, not just satisfaction scores
- Recommendations balanced across customer satisfaction AND operational efficiency
- Track multiple metrics to get complete picture

### POV 4: [Your Service Type] Leadership Beats Full-Time Hire for Series A Companies

> "Why hire one person's 10 years of experience when you can access 15+ years fractionally?"

**My Take**: Series A SaaS companies (50-200 customers) get better ROI from [your service type] consultant than full-time [Field] hire.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "You need a full-time [Field] leader at Series A."

**Why Fractional Often Wins**:
- More experience for the budget ($8-12K/month fractional vs $150K+ full-time)
- Flexibility to scale hours up/down with needs
- No recruiting timeline (3-6 months to hire, start immediately with fractional)
- Fresh perspective from working with multiple companies
- Lower risk if fit isn't perfect

**When Full-Time Makes Sense**:
- 200+ customers and high complexity
- Need 30+ hours/week of [Field] leadership consistently
- Strategic importance warrants full-time focus
- Company culture values in-house leadership

**Implications for My Work**:
- Positioning emphasizes fractional advantages for Series A stage
- Help clients understand when to transition to full-time
- Design engagements that prove value quickly

### POV 5: Knowledge Bases Are Overbuilt

> "Start with 20 articles, not 200."

**My Take**: Companies waste time building comprehensive knowledge bases when 20-30 targeted articles would solve 70% of tickets.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "We need a complete knowledge base covering everything."

**Why That's Wrong**:
- 80/20 rule: 20% of questions are 80% of volume
- Comprehensive KBs are never maintained (go stale quickly)
- Perfect is the enemy of done - 20 articles NOW better than 200 articles in 6 months
- Customers don't want 200 articles - they want the answer to their specific question

**The Right Approach**:
- Start with ticket analysis - what are top 20 questions?
- Write 20-30 articles covering those questions
- Measure deflection rate
- Add articles based on data about what's still getting tickets
- Update and maintain what exists before adding more

**Implications for My Work**:
- KB recommendations start with data about ticket patterns
- Initial scope: 20-30 articles, not comprehensive coverage
- Emphasize maintenance over comprehensive coverage
- Track which articles actually get used

### POV 6: Your Best Support Hire Is Often Underqualified on Paper

> "I'd rather hire for empathy and coach technical skills than hire for tech knowledge and try to teach empathy."

**My Take**: Hiring for soft skills (empathy, communication, problem-solving) and training product knowledge beats hiring for product expertise.

**Conventional Wisdom Says**: "We need someone with [our industry] experience."

**Why That Limits You**:
- Product knowledge can be taught in 2-4 weeks
- Empathy and customer service mentality can't
- Industry experience candidates often want more money
- Great support person from different industry brings fresh perspective

**What To Optimize For**:
- Communication skills (written and verbal)
- Problem-solving ability
- Genuine desire to help people
- Learning agility
- Then train on product

**Implications for My Work**:
- Hiring support emphasizes soft skills + learning ability
- Onboarding includes intensive product training
- Don't filter out great candidates for lack of industry experience
